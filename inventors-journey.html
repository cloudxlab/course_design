The Journey

From first principles to a movement

Every chapter of this story is a clue to why the course exists — and why it works the way it does.

College Years

A student discovers what computing really is

While his classmates treated programming as one of many subjects to get through, Sandeep couldn't stop. He studied every aspect of computing — not for grades, but because each idea led to another, deeper one. Operating systems. Algorithms. Data structures. Computer architecture. He wasn't building a CV. He was building a model of the world inside his head.

The real insight came from going beyond the syllabus — from asking not just what an algorithm does, but why it was designed that way. That question would define everything that followed.

First principles thinking
2002

D.E. Shaw — the very first hire

In 2002, Sandeep joined D.E. Shaw, one of the most selective quantitative investment firms in the world — a company that hired fewer engineers than most IIT departments graduated. Getting in required exactly the kind of thinking he had spent college developing: the ability to reason from scratch, not recall from memory.

He wasn't handed a playbook. He built systems. He solved problems that had no known answers. And he learned, early, that the engineers who thrived were the ones who had trained themselves to think — not just to execute.

D.E. Shaw, 2002
Entrepreneurship

The first company — tBits

The entrepreneur in him couldn't stay dormant. Sandeep founded tBits, channelling his technical depth into building something from the ground up. Starting a company is a different kind of education — every decision is made without a safety net, every design choice has consequences. It is, in many ways, the purest form of learning by inventing.

Founder
Industry

InMobi — scale, data, and machine intelligence

At InMobi, Sandeep worked inside one of India's first global tech unicorns — at the intersection of data, scale, and machine learning long before ML became a buzzword in Indian tech. He saw how the most impactful engineers weren't the ones who knew the most frameworks. They were the ones who understood the underlying mathematics well enough to know when frameworks were wrong.

InMobi
Industry

Amazon — engineering at the world's highest standard

Amazon was the final, and perhaps most instructive, chapter of Sandeep's industry career. Amazon's engineering culture is famously first-principles — decisions are justified, systems are questioned, and seniority earns you the right to be challenged more, not less. It was a culture Sandeep recognised immediately, because it matched the way he had always thought.

It also crystallised a growing frustration: the engineers who struggled at Amazon weren't the ones who lacked intelligence. They were the ones whose education had trained them to reproduce answers — not to generate them.

Amazon
The Realisation

A gap no one was closing

Across D.E. Shaw, InMobi, and Amazon, Sandeep hired engineers, mentored new graduates, and watched the same pattern repeat itself. Brilliant students. Strong marks. Completely unprepared. They had memorised textbooks and survived exams — but when a problem had no answer key, they froze.

The gap wasn't talent. It wasn't effort. It was the method. Indian education had perfected the delivery of information. It had entirely neglected the development of thinking.

The turning point

"Formulas are forgettable. What you invent — you own. I had seen too many engineers who could recite algorithms but couldn't design one. That's not a talent problem. That's a teaching problem."

— Sandeep Giri, Founder, CloudxLab
💡

Education had a design flaw. Sandeep decided to fix it.

The most in-demand engineering roles in the world — at companies like Google, Amazon, D.E. Shaw, and every AI-first startup — require one thing above all else: the ability to think through a problem no one has seen before. Yet every course, every bootcamp, and every textbook was optimised for the opposite: teaching known answers to known questions. Sandeep didn't want to build another course. He wanted to build a different kind of engineer.

CloudxLab — Phase 1

A hands-on learning platform, built from the ground up

The first thing Sandeep and Abhinav agreed on: no passive video watching. The platform they built required learners to actually write code, run it in the cloud, and get feedback — immediately, without setup. No local environment. No installation friction. Just a problem, a browser, and the requirement to solve it.

This was 2014. Browser-based cloud labs for learning to code were not yet standard. CloudxLab was one of the first to make them work reliably at scale.

Hands-on lab · Cloud-native
CloudxLab — Phase 2

Gamification — making persistence feel like play

Learning to code is hard. Most people quit not because the material is too difficult, but because the feedback loop is too slow. You put in effort; you don't know if it's working; you lose momentum.

Sandeep and Abhinav added gamification components — streaks, badges, leaderboards, instant automated feedback — to make progress visible and the next step obvious. The goal was not to make it easier. The goal was to make it impossible to stop.

Gamification · Automated feedback
The Evolution

Good wasn't good enough — the method kept improving

CloudxLab grew. The platform worked. Students were learning. But Sandeep kept noticing the same thing: students who completed the platform could use tools, but still struggled when a problem looked different from what they had practised. The platform was teaching skills. He wanted it to teach thinking.

Over years of iteration — watching students, adjusting problems, reordering modules, changing the sequencing of explanation and challenge — a new method emerged: problem first, explanation second. Give students a problem they have never seen. Let them struggle. Then, and only then, reveal the concept. The struggle is not the obstacle. The struggle is the learning.

Invent-to-Learn method
Today

The Inventor's Journey — learning by inventing

The course that emerged from all of this is not a collection of video lectures. It is a structured, 8-month programme in which every concept is first presented as a problem you have never been given the answer to. You design. You build. You defend. And then — only after you have invented your own version — you discover what the world calls it and why it was designed that way.

By the end, students don't know algorithms. They own them. And that is a different kind of knowledge entirely — the kind that doesn't disappear under pressure, that adapts when the problem changes, and that makes an interviewer lean forward rather than move on.

The Method

Why it works — three ideas Sandeep never stopped believing

🔬
Struggle before explanation

When you encounter a concept before you have been told what it is, your brain creates a genuine need for it. The explanation lands differently. It becomes the answer to a question you were already asking.

🏗️
Build it before you use it

Anyone can import a library. But the person who has built a neural network from NumPy sees the library differently — as a convenience on top of something they already understand. That is a different level of confidence.

🎤
Defend what you invented

Explaining your solution out loud — to an instructor, to a peer, in a mock interview — is the highest form of encoding. If you can defend it, you own it. If you can't, you've identified the gap before it costs you.

♾️
Transferable forever

Programming languages change. Frameworks get deprecated. But the ability to decompose a new problem into solvable pieces — that is a skill you carry into every role, every year, for the rest of your career.
